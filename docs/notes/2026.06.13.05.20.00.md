## The Core Pivot: What Changes

The existing [gates-of-aker](https://github.com/octave-commons/gates-of-aker) is built around a web frontend (`web/`, `node_modules/`, shadow-cljs), a Clojure backend serving HTTP, and ClojureScript rendering in-browser. The new system eliminates every layer that exists to serve a browser. The simulation becomes the primary artifact. The "renderer" is a terminal UI — [Lanterna](https://github.com/mabe02/lanterna) via the JVM, or a custom ANSI/TUI layer using pure Clojure — displaying a 3D world projected onto a terminal viewport. The player is inside the sim, not observing an HTTP API.

***

## Architecture: Four Namespaces

Following house rules, strict separation into four categories:

```
src/
  domain/          ← Pure physics, biology, civilization logic. Zero I/O.
  infra/           ← Rendering, persistence (EDN/nippy), input dispatch.
  shape/           ← Coordinate transforms, geometry, projection math.
  law/             ← Malli schemas, contract validators, guards.
```

No `utils/`. No junk drawers. Every cross-boundary call names a Malli validator.

***

## The World Model: Category of Positions

The planet is a unit sphere. All positions are spherical coordinates `[φ θ r]` (latitude, longitude, altitude). This is not optional — a flat 2D grid cannot represent a globe without seam artifacts, and you need tides, day/night terminator, and atmosphere to behave physically correctly.

```clojure
;; shape/sphere.clj
(defn ^{:doc "Spherical -> Cartesian. Pure morphism, domain-agnostic."}
  sph->cart
  [[φ θ r]]
  [(* r (Math/cos φ) (Math/cos θ))
   (* r (Math/cos φ) (Math/sin θ))
   (* r (Math/sin φ))])

(defn ^{:doc "Angular great-circle distance between two sphere points."}
  angular-distance
  [[φ1 θ1] [φ2 θ2]]
  (Math/acos (+ (* (Math/sin φ1) (Math/sin φ2))
                (* (Math/cos φ1) (Math/cos φ2) (Math/cos (- θ2 θ1))))))
```

## The LOD Engine: Only Calculate What Matters

This is the hard problem you named — simulating civilizations without dying on CPU. The solution is **level-of-detail simulation zones** driven by the player's position. Think of it as three concentric shells:

| Zone | Radius | Tick rate | What runs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Immediate** | ~5 km | Every frame (60 Hz) | Full physics, individual agents, weather cells |
| **Regional** | ~500 km | Every 1 s | Statistical agent summaries, coarse weather, trade flows |
| **Global** | Whole planet | Every 60 s | Civilization-level bookkeeping, epoch-scale events |

The mechanism is a dispatch table keyed on `zone-id` → `tick-fn`. Each zone function receives only the data relevant to its scale. Zones promote/demote as the player moves. This maps naturally to Clojure's `core.async` pipeline or, better, to explicit `agent`-per-zone with a coordinator.

```clojure
;; domain/lod.clj
(defn zone-for-position
  "Classify a world position relative to the player anchor."
  [player-pos world-pos]
  (let [d (angular-distance player-pos world-pos)
        d-km (* d 6371)]        ; planet radius configurable
    (cond
      (< d-km 5)    :immediate
      (< d-km 500)  :regional
      :else          :global)))
```

Civilizations in the `:global` zone don't run agent-by-agent — they run as a **summary aggregate**: birth rate, death rate, resource delta, expansion vector. They are fully realized only when the player's zone boundary crosses into them. This is the "things happening beyond the view" effect you want: the world was always computing at low fidelity, and when you arrive, it snaps into crisp local detail as if it never stopped.

## The Solar System: Actual Orbital Mechanics

Day/night, seasons, tides, and moon phases all derive from a single source of truth: the orbital state vector.

```clojure
;; domain/orbital.clj
(defrecord OrbitalBody
  [name mass semi-major-axis eccentricity inclination
   longitude-of-ascending-node argument-of-periapsis true-anomaly])

(defn solar-angle
  "Returns the angle between a surface point and the sun direction at sim-time t."
  [planet moon t surface-point]
  ;; Kepler's equation integrated forward from t=0
  ;; → sun-direction in planet-body frame
  ;; → dot product with surface-normal at surface-point
  ,,,)
```

From `solar-angle` flows everything: temperature (insolation model), surface illumination for rendering, and the terminator line that separates the lit hemisphere. The moon(s) use the same `OrbitalBody` record — tidal force is the gradient of the moon's gravitational field across the planet's diameter, trivial to compute once you have positions.

## Atmosphere & Water Cycle

The atmosphere runs as a **cellular automaton on the sphere**. Subdivide the sphere into a geodesic grid (icosphere subdivision gives you uniform-area cells with no pole singularity). Each cell carries:

```clojure
;; domain/atmosphere.clj
(defrecord AtmosCell
  [temperature    ; K
   pressure       ; Pa
   humidity       ; 0.0–1.0
   wind-velocity  ; [u v] tangent-plane m/s
   cloud-cover    ; 0.0–1.0
   precipitation]) ; mm/hour
```

Each tick, cells exchange heat and moisture with their six icosphere neighbors via the discretized shallow-water equations. You don't need full Navier-Stokes — the Primitive Equations approximation used in real atmospheric models gives you realistic weather at manageable cost. The key insight: **cloud cover and precipitation are emergent**, not scripted. Moisture rises when insolation heats the surface, advects with wind, condenses when cells cool at altitude. Rainfall feeds rivers, rivers carve geography, geography channels wind.

## Biology: The Ecological Web

Plants and animals operate as populations on the same geodesic grid cells, not as individual agents (except in the `:immediate` zone). Each cell carries a `BiomeCell`:

```clojure
;; domain/biome.clj
(defrecord BiomeCell
  [flora-mass     ; kg/km²
   fauna-mass     ; kg/km²
   soil-moisture  ; 0.0–1.0
   nutrients      ; 0.0–1.0
   species-counts ; {species-id -> population}
  ])
```

Lotka-Volterra dynamics govern predator-prey relationships. Climate data from `AtmosCell` drives growth rates — cold and dry kills flora, flora collapse cascades to fauna starvation. This is the engine that makes the world feel alive: civilizations that over-farm their cell die; cities in flood plains get wiped by weather events that the atmosphere simulation generated independently.

## Civilizations as Emergent Agents

Civilizations are not scripted factions — they are large populations of agents sharing a belief system (a `Culture` record) and organized around resources. Individual agents in the `:regional` and `:global` zones are collapsed into the civilization aggregate; they only individuate in the `:immediate` zone.

```clojure
;; domain/civilization.clj
(defrecord Civilization
  [id name culture
   territory      ; set of cell-ids
   population     ; long
   resource-stocks ; {resource-id -> quantity}
   tech-level     ; 0.0–10.0
   expansion-pressure]) ; drives territory spread

(defn tick-civilization
  "Pure domain step. No I/O."
  [civ world-state dt]
  (-> civ
      (consume-resources world-state dt)
      (grow-population dt)
      (expand-territory world-state)
      (advance-technology dt)))
```

The civilizations don't "know" the player exists. They react to their environment — biome conditions, other civilizations, natural disasters. The "story from the beginning of life" emerges because you can start the simulation with only microbial life in the `BiomeCell` populations and run forward until multicellular organisms, then fauna, then sapient agents cross an emergence threshold.

## Rendering: Terminal 3D

Without a web frontend, you render to a terminal. The options, in order of depth:

1. **[Charterm](https://clojars.org/charterm)** — pure Clojure ANSI terminal control, good for cursor-addressable 2D maps.
2. **[Lanterna via Java interop](https://github.com/mabe02/lanterna)** — full terminal GUI with windowing, color, and resize handling. The right choice.
3. **ASCII raycast / sphere projection** — project the 3D globe onto a 2D terminal viewport using a raycasting pass: for each terminal cell, cast a ray from the camera through the cell, intersect with the sphere, look up the biome/atmosphere/civilization at that intersection point, and render the appropriate character + ANSI color.

The raycast approach gives you genuine 3D — you can orbit the camera around the planet, zoom in to see continents, and the day/night terminator renders as a visible shadow line moving in real time.

```clojure
;; infra/render.clj
(defn render-globe
  "Cast rays through terminal viewport onto planet sphere."
  [{:keys [camera planet atmos biome]} terminal-size]
  (let [{:keys [width height]} terminal-size]
    (for [row (range height)
          col (range width)
          :let [ray     (camera/pixel->ray camera col row width height)
                hit     (sphere/ray-intersect ray (:radius planet))
                surface (when hit (sphere/cart->sph hit))
                cell-id (when surface (grid/sph->cell surface))]]
      (render-cell cell-id atmos biome))))
```

Each `render-cell` maps the biome + atmosphere + civilization state to a character and color. Forests are `♣` in green. Desert is `·` in ochre. Cities glow amber. Clouds are `≋` in white. The ocean is `~` in blue-grey varying with depth and wave state.

## The "Off-Screen World" Effect

This is the key experiential goal — the sense that the world doesn't stop when you're not watching. Three mechanisms produce it:

1. **Continuous low-fidelity ticking** — the global and regional zones run on independent threads (`agent` or `virtual-thread`), updating civilization aggregates and atmosphere coarsely while the player is elsewhere.
2. **Event journaling** — every significant event (civilization war declaration, major storm, species extinction, volcanic eruption) is appended to an event log with a timestamp and location. When the player travels to a new area, recent events in that area's log are surfaced as discoverable history ("The ruins here were abandoned 40 years ago during the Drought of the Second Sun").
3. **State interpolation on arrival** — when the player enters a new region, the simulation "catches up" the `:immediate` zone by running a fast-forward pass from the last known regional state, applying accumulated delta ticks at accelerated time. The player doesn't see a loading screen — they see smoke still rising from a battle that ended while they were elsewhere.

## Epistemic Contracts: First Tests to Write

Before any implementation, the μ (specs) come first:

```clojure
;; test/domain/orbital_test.clj
(deftest solar-angle-contract
  ;; FACT: solar angle at subsolar point = 0 (sun directly overhead)
  ;; FACT: solar angle at antipode = π (sun directly below)
  (is (< (solar-angle subsolar-point t) 0.001))
  (is (> (solar-angle antipodal-point t) (- Math/PI 0.001))))

(deftest lod-zone-test
  ;; FACT: a point 3km away is :immediate
  ;; FACT: a point 600km away is :global
  (is (= :immediate (zone-for-position [0 0] (offset-point [0 0] 3))))
  (is (= :global    (zone-for-position [0 0] (offset-point [0 0] 600)))))
```

Red first. The failing test is a provisional observation. The passing test is a validated fact.
